Ohm's Law

Ohm’s law states the relationship between electric current and potential difference. The current that flows through most conductors is directly proportional to the voltage applied to it. Georg Simon Ohm, a German physicist was the first to verify Ohm’s law experimentally.

Formula in Series Circuit:

Rtotal = R1 + R2 + . . . Rn (The total equivalent resistance of a series circuit is equal to the sum of the individual resistances)

Itotal = I1 = I2 = . . . In (All components in a series circuit conduct the same current)

Vtotal = V1 + V2 + . . . Vn (The total voltage drop in a series circuit is equal to the sum of the individual voltage drops)

V = IR (Voltage = Current × Resistance)

Applications

When the other two numbers are known, Ohm’s law may be used to determine the voltage, current, impedance, or resistance of a linear electric circuit.

Main applications of Ohm’s Law:

1. It also simplifies power calculations.
2. To keep the desired voltage drop between the electrical components, Ohm’s law is employed.
3. An electric circuit’s voltage, resistance, or current must be determined.
4. Ohm’s law is also utilised to redirect current in DC ammeters and other DC shunts.
